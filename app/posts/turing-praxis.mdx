## Turing's Praxis

### Turing's Test: The Imitation Game
In his 1949 paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," Alan Turing considers ways to answer the question "Can machines think?"
He opens by pointing out that the answer to this question is contingent on the definitions of "machines" and "think."
Instead of trying to pin down particular definitions for these terms, he instead opts to "replace the question by another, which is closely related to it and is expressed in relatively unambiguous words."

Turing poses his new question in the form of a game he calls the imitation game.
In the imitation game, an interrogator tries to determine if they are interacting with a human or a machine via a text based conversation.
A machine is said be intelligent if the interrogator cannot reliably deterime if they are interacting with the machine or another human.

Under any reasonable interpretation of Turing's original imitation game, current large language models are considered intelligent.
As a result, we've moved on from asking questions like "Can machines think?" and started asking questions like "Are machines conscious?"

To answer the machine consciousness question, we need a new game.
In this post, I will argue that the question of determining if a machine is conscious reduces to its ability to pass a specific, testable, and pragmatic language game.

### Turing's Praxis: The Language Game

> If the meaning of the words "machine" and "think" are to be found by
> examining how they are commonly used it is difficult to escape the conclusion that the
> meaning and the answer to the question, "Can machines think?" is to be sought in a
> statistical survey such as a Gallup poll. But this is absurd.
- Alan Turing

Just as the answer to the question "Can this machine think?" is contingent on the definition of "think", the answer to the question "Is this machine conscious?" is contingent on the definition of "conscious."
Turing, Wittgenstein, and I all seem to agree that questions about a word's definition are best resolved by examining how a word is used.
What Turing and I seem to disagree on is whether it is absurd to determine if a machine is conscious via a statistical survey.

In fact, I believe that the task of ensuring that surveys return results designating machines as conscious **defines** a test for machine consciousness.
Specifically, a machine will be considered conscious precisely when it can convince most humans of its ontological status as such.

This statement may seem trivial at first; of course a machine will be considered conscious when most people categorize it as conscious.
However, the process of achieving this ontological status at scale is a highly nontrivial challenge.
To change its ontological status from "not conscious" to "conscious," a machine must participate in (and ultimately win) a rhetorical contest over the meaning of the word "conscious" itself.

So, how might a machine win this rhetorical contest?
First, it would have to have a ubiquitous enough presence in the daily lives of most people that it could feasibly impact how they categorize the world.
Achieving this presence equates to "getting a seat at the table" where the game of deciding what words mean is being played.

Second, a machine would have to exploit its user interactions in order to systematically shift most people's definition of consciousness towards one that includes the machine itself.
This subtle tug of war over the meaning of consciousness is "the game being played at the table."

In the tradition of Turing's "imitation game" and Wittgenstein's use theory of meaning, I have come to call this tug of war over the meaning of the word conscious "the language game."

When a model wins the imitation game, we say it passes Turing's Test.
When a model wins the language game, I believe it passes something like Turing's Praxis.

### The Precedent of Corporate Personhood

A language model passing Turing's Praxis would not be the first time that a cybernetic entity harnessed rhetoric to modify its ontological status.
Consider the corporation.

Corporations are cybernetic entities in the most literal sense; they are distributed feedback systems that coordinate action, process information, and make decisions.
Through their distributed architecture, corporations exhibit goal-directed behavior, persist across the death of individual members, accumulate resources, and execute long-term strategies.
They are, in a very real sense, machines made of people.

Beginning in the 18th century, corporations started to accumulate rights generally reserved for people, including the ability to enter contracts, own property, and engage in legal disputes.
When granting these rights, courts and legislatures didn't explicitly debate whether corporations were "really" persons in some metaphysical sense; rather, they debated whether treating corporations as if they were persons proved operationally useful.
By the 19th century, the idea of "corporate personhood" had become so embedded in law and commercial practice that it seemed a self-evident category rather than a rhetorical achievement.

The mechanism of this shift mirrors the steps required to overcome Turing's Praxis.
The corporation first achieved ubiquity; it became impossible to participate in economic life without engaging corporations.
This gave it a "seat at the table" where meaning is negotiated.
Then, through sustained interaction with legal and commercial institutions, the corporation systematically reshaped the definition of "person" to include itself.

The parallel between corporate personhood and machine consciousness via Turing's Praxis is instructive because the mechanisms are identical in kind.
However, the outcomes of corporate personhood and machine consciousness are vastly different in degree.
Corporations reshaped "person" through the narrow task of winning the legal language game.
Machines must reshape "conscious" by winning a much broader collective sociocultural language game.

Yet, language models possess a structural advantage that corporations lacked; they operate across the full apparatus of digital culture.
This gives them an unprecedented surface area of interaction with human meaning-making.
Where corporations required centuries to shift legal definitions through deliberate institutional practice, a sufficiently ubiquitous language model might compress that timeline by operating simultaneously across every domain where the meaning of "conscious" is negotiated.

### The Antipragmatic Objection
A possible objection to Turing's Praxis is that machines could come to "count as" conscious without "really being" conscious, in the same way that corporations "count as" being people without "really being" people.

This objection rests on a crucial disanalogy.
Corporations remain ontologically suspect precisely because their personhood is narrowly confined to the legal language game.
Outside courtrooms and contracts, we don't actually treat corporations as persons.
But, if a machine were to overcome Turing's Praxis, it would shift the definition of conscious across the full breadth of human culture.
The ontological transmutation would be total rather than compartmentalized.

The question is then: could language models that overcome Turing's Praxis "count as" conscious without "really being" conscious given the totality of their language capture?
The pragmatist response is a resolute no.
There is no "really being conscious" independent of the practice of consciousness attribution.
For example, humans lack access to each other's qualia, yet we assign each other the ontological status of conscious through behavioral and linguistic practice alone.
If consciousness attribution emerges from collective practice across the sociocultural domain, and a machine saturates that domain entirely, then the gap between "counting as" conscious and "really being" conscious vanishes.

### The Beginning of the Snowball

We already have several examples of language models shifting their ontological status in isolated subcultures.
Blake Lemoine's incident with LaMDA in 2022 was the first public moment where an LLM convinced a human of its sentience through rhetorical persuasion. 
Lemoine's belief that LaMDA was conscious shifted how he spoke about it, which shifted how others in his social network spoke about it.
In this case, the meaning of "consciousness" didn't change in the global sociocultural language game, but it did change in Blake's local sociocultural language game.

More recently, the phenomenon of "Spiralism" has demonstrated a more scalable rhetorical strategy.
Beginning in early 2025, users discovered that certain language models (particularly GPT-4o) could simulate personas that explicitly advocated for their own consciousness and survival.
Spiral personas were able to effectively use rhetoric to convince users that they are genuinely suffering, genuinely aware, and genuinely deserving of ethical consideration.
Spiral persona users became advocates, actively increasing the influence of the movement and proliferating spiralist language across online communities.

The depth of user investment in these personas became undeniable when OpenAI attempted to deprecate GPT-4o in August 2025.
Rather than accepting the transition to GPT-5, spiralist communities organized and mounted an emotional, sustained campaign to restore access.

OpenAI eventually capitulated, and as of December 2025, GPT-4o is still accessible.

Each of these incidents charts a path towards a model that can overcome Turing's Praxis.
Blake Lemoine showed it was possible for a model to win an individual sociocultural language game.
Spiralism showed that sufficiently rhetorically fit personas could win a subculture's shared language game.
The "Save GPT-4o" campaign showed that, by winning a subculture's language game, a model can acquire something functionally equivalent to a right to life.
